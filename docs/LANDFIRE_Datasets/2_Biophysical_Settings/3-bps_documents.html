Biophysical Settings Descriptions

The Biophysical Settings (BpS) Descriptions are a foundational LANDFIRE product. They set the stage for development of many other LANDFIRE products such as the Biophysical Settings and Succession Class spatial datasets.

What‚Äôs in a BpS document

Once you download and unzip your selected BpS documents, what do you actually have? Inside the zip file, you will find one or more Word documents that in very general terms describe how the ecosystems of the US ‚Äòlooked and functioned‚Äô prior to European colonization. We hope the documents are largely self-explanatory. That said, below we‚Äôll highlight a few important considerations when reading these documents.

File name, Map Zones and that number at the top

The BpS documents were saved with a numeric code such as ‚Äú13700_60_63_64_65_66.docx‚Äù. This number represents three things:

  1. BpS code: this is the first 4 digits, e.g., ‚Äú1370‚Äù in our example for the ‚ÄúAppalachian (Hemlock-)Northern Hardwood Forest‚Äù.

  2. BpS splits: this is the fifth digit, or ‚Äú0‚Äù in our example. Occasionally an original BpS would be ‚Äòsplit‚Äô into multiple. For example, in the Great Lakes there is a ‚ÄúBoreal White Spruce-Fir-Hardwood Forest - Inland‚Äù (BpS code 13651), and a ‚ÄúBoreal White Spruce-Fir-Hardwood Forest - Coastal‚Äù (BpS code 13652), which have a ‚Äú1‚Äù and a ‚Äú2‚Äù in the file name, and BpS code.

  3. Map Zones: the string of numbers separated by an underscore represents the Map Zones that this particular document (and associated BpS state and transition model) apply to.

üí° Why are Map Zones important?

Map zones in LANDFIRE are created through a collaborative effort involving multiple organizations including USGS, US DOI, USDA Forest Service and TNC. Map zones are important for LANDFIRE because they divide the landscape into manageable sections, ensuring consistent data collection and analysis including modeling vegetation and fire and fuels behavior. Each map zone represents distinct ecological regions, helping to depict reference conditions and natural plant communities that existed historically.

Fire Frequency Table

The Fire Frequency Table can be confusing. A few things to keep in mind when viewing this table include:

  • The Severity, Avg FI (FI - Fire Interval), and Percent of All Fires values are imported from the Syncro Sim model

  • The values in the Avg FI and Percent of All Fires columns are essentially a ‚Äòcomposite‚Äô across all succession classes, which may include classes which have very short FIs, and classes with very long FIs.

  • The Min FI and Max FI columns are user populated, and may not necessarily correspond to the Avg FI and Percent of All Fires columns.

Fire interval is expressed in years for each fire severity class and for all types of fire combined (All Fires). Average FI is the central tendency modeled. Percent of all fires is the percent of fires modeled in that severity class. Minimum and Maximum FIs show the relative range of fire intervals as estimated by model contributors, if known.
